### Core vs Non-Core Differentiation

- Core Area: Most resilient; scarce resources, employment density, school and transit access support liquidity, but valuation is high.
- Mature Urban Area: More stable than outer suburbs; suitable units may hold value, but upside needs transaction confirmation.
- Outer Suburb: More sensitive to listing pressure, commute cost, and weak liquidity; avoid assuming core-area resilience applies here.
- New District: Needs proof of job and population import; speculative demand should be treated cautiously.
- Satellite City: More exposed to commute, supply, and policy expectation changes; long holding period and liquidity risk should be emphasized.

### Self-Use Recommendation

- Recommendation: Selective acceptable for long holding period buyers in core or mature urban areas.
- Reason: Self-use value can be justified by commute, school, hospital, family stability, and scarce services even when investment return is modest.
- Suitable Buyer Type: Stable income household, low leverage ratio, 5-10 year holding period, clear location need.

### Investment Recommendation

- Recommendation: Conservative; avoid high-leverage investment and avoid low-liquidity outer-suburb units.
- Reason: Rental yield is usually low relative to total price, and resale demand is more selective.
- Leverage Warning: Do not rely on short-term policy easing to cover weak rental yield or uncertain resale liquidity.

### Risks and Data Gaps

- Main Risks: Missing latest transaction/listing evidence, high affordability pressure, district divergence, listing pressure in non-core areas.
- Data Gaps: This is an example only; it does not use verified latest transaction, listing, rental yield, or district-level inventory data.
- Notes on conflicting signals: Population and high-salary jobs support medium-long-term core demand, but economic expectations and affordability pressure limit short-term upside.
